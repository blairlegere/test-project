Unified Search, Rewritten for Human Networks

Overview
A unified relationship intelligence interface, designed to provide a seamless experience when searching Blackstone’s firm-wide network.
Role
Design Lead
Date Range
September 2024 - December 2024

As data environments grow in complexity, how do you design an entry point that enables reliable discovery?

The Challenge

Across an investment firm, deal teams regularly ask questions like:

  • Who can make a warm introduction to the CEO of Microsoft?
  • When was the last time we spoke with Rover?
  • Who has the longest-running relationship with Jersey Mike’s?

Answering these questions, however, required manual effort across fragmented systems which differed across teams.

Enter Rolodex: Blackstone’s relationship‑intelligence platform surfacing people, companies, and their internal employee connections.

Over time, multiple data sources were centralized to build richer, more holistic profiles and search capabilities. As the dataset matured, so did the need for a smarter search interface.

Role

I led the end‑to‑end redesign of Rolodex Search, from research through implementation.

My work included:

  • User research across business units
  • UI/UX design spanning Low and high-fidelity prototyping
  • Project managing front‑end development
  • Design QA testing and engineering collaboration

The Problem

Rolodex’s search interface was its primary entry point—and its primary point of failure. Teams needed a fast, reliable way to:

  • Find people or companies
  • Understand mutual connections
  • Disambiguate entities with shared or overlapping names

The existing interface required users to decide whether they were searching for a Person or Company before typing—a decision they often couldn’t make.

A query like “Morgan” exposed the flaw immediately. Was the user looking for a first or last name? A company? Something else entirely?

Moreover, incomplete profiles, inconsistent naming, and a constrained search model made it difficult to confidently surface the “correct” result.

How do you return the most useful answer when intent is ambiguous and relationships—not only keywords—define relevance?

User Research

In order to determine the requirements for optimal search, I conducted research across teams, business units, and seniority levels to afford a holistic approach.

  • Designed a testing plan for user profiles spanning cross-firm initiatives: deal sourcing, human capital management, and corporate finance.
  • Moderated testing sessions gathering qualitative user experience and pain points.
  • Iterated low and high fidelity prototypes based on user feedback.

Insight

The autocomplete dropdown became the first opportunity to orient users with the dataset. Surfacing potential matches across categories and pairing them with strong, identifiable visual cues—logos, URLs, locations—reduced cognitive load before users ever committed to a result.

Search results needed to be previewable. Through research, we identified a small set of signals that helped users quickly assess relevance: total connections, notable employees, geography, and associated industries.

During testing, users were frequently entering queries like “real estate” or “database engineering.” They weren’t searching for a company or a person—they were searching for experience.

Rather than correct this behavior, we embraced it by integrating Expert Search into the search results page.

Solution

Unified Search

We removed the People/Company toggle and introduced a single, unified search that interprets intent after the query, not before it. Results span people, companies, and expertise within one cohesive experience.

Relevance‑Driven Ranking

Based on search history analysis and behavioral research, results were prioritized using the following signals:

  • Spelling and character closeness
  • Internal employee status
  • Number of Blackstone connections
  • Profile completeness

This balanced precision with practicality in a noisy, real‑world dataset.

Scannable Results

We designed a dynamic autocomplete dropdown to support fast disambiguation, paired with a unified results page that clearly surfaced connection context and provided direct access to Expert AI.

Expertise Search

Integrating this separate Rolodex tool into the search results enabled users to identify internal experts based on the depth and volume of their external correspondences within a given industry, turning relationship data into a proxy for real‑world experience.
